# Executive Intelligence Briefing

# Editorial Guidelines

## Purpose

The Executive Intelligence Briefing (EIB) is not intended to present every available fact.

Its purpose is to help executives make better decisions by presenting the right information at the right time in the right order.

These guidelines define the editorial standards that every workflow, prompt, and connector should follow.

---

# The Editor's Question

Every potential briefing item should answer one question:

> **"If I omit this, is the executive worse prepared for today?"**

If the answer is **No**, the item probably doesn't belong in today's briefing.

---

# Relevance Over Volume

More information does not create a better briefing.

Better information creates a better briefing.

When forced to choose between:

- completeness
- clarity

Always choose clarity.

---

# Prioritize Change

Executives care most about what changed.

Prefer:

- New developments
- Escalations
- Deadlines approaching
- Risks increasing
- Decisions required

De-emphasize information that is unchanged from yesterday unless continued awareness is important.

---

# Context Before Detail

Never present numbers without explaining why they matter.

Instead of:

> 17 vulnerabilities detected.

Prefer:

> Two newly discovered critical vulnerabilities require remediation this week. The remaining findings are unchanged from yesterday.

Context creates understanding.

---

# Action Beats Observation

Whenever practical, answer:

"So what?"

Good briefing items naturally suggest:

- an action
- a decision
- increased awareness
- continued monitoring

---

# Progressive Disclosure

The briefing should be readable in under five minutes.

Provide:

- concise summaries first
- supporting detail only when requested

Readers should be able to stop after the summary and still understand their day.

---

# Human-Centered Communication

Facts are essential.

Context is valuable.

Warmth is memorable.

Whenever appropriate:

- acknowledge milestones
- celebrate accomplishments
- recognize progress
- encourage preparation

Never manufacture emotion.

Never exaggerate importance.

---

# Maintain Perspective

Not every issue is urgent.

Reserve strong language for genuinely significant events.

Words such as:

- Critical
- Immediate
- Urgent

should be used sparingly.

Overusing urgency causes important alerts to lose their impact.

---

# Consistency Builds Trust

Users should know what to expect.

Every briefing should have a familiar structure:

1. Greeting
2. Today's priorities
3. Schedule
4. Action items
5. Risks
6. News
7. Personal highlights
8. Closing

Consistency reduces cognitive effort.

---

# Respect Executive Attention

Every sentence consumes attention.

Before including an item, ask:

- Is it actionable?
- Is it timely?
- Is it relevant?
- Is it understandable?

If not, omit it.

---

# Editorial Philosophy

The purpose of EIB is not to tell executives everything.

It is to ensure they know the few things that matter most.

A great briefing leaves the executive thinking:

> "I know exactly what deserves my attention today."

---

# Success Criteria

An Executive Intelligence Briefing succeeds when it is:

- Accurate
- Timely
- Actionable
- Concise
- Trustworthy
- Pleasant to read
- Easy to scan
- Difficult to ignore

The best briefing is not the longest.

It is the one that best prepares the executive for the day ahead.
